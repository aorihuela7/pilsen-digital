Pilsen Digital

Cartography of Resistance, Language & Identity

A digital humanities project exploring Chicago's Mexican neighborhood

Author: Alejandro Orihuela

Mentor: Dr. María Cecilia Lozada

Research Framework

Project Context

This project investigates the sociocultural landscape of Pilsen, Chicago, analyzing how Mexican-origin communities negotiate their identity and belonging in the face of gentrification and urban displacement. It focuses on the neighborhood's "archaeology"—including murals, oral histories, and linguistic markers—as a space of collective memory and symbolic resistance.

The project stems from the need to connect academic Spanish language instruction with the lived realities of local Spanish-speaking communities, fostering not only linguistic competence but also historical awareness and community engagement among students.

Research Question

How can a web-based repository for University of Chicago students, focused on Pilsen, illuminate overlooked layers of the neighborhood's past and connect them to present-day experiences of place and belonging?

Significance

This research is vital given the rapid socioeconomic changes that threaten to erase the historical character of Chicago's most iconic Mexican neighborhood. By linking Latin American and Caribbean Studies with the digital realm, the project addresses the "archival silence" surrounding migrant spaces, contributing to scholarly debates on "counter-mapping" and the social production of urban space.

Key Findings

The research identified three fundamental patterns regarding the life of the Latino community in Chicago:

01

Visibility vs. Displacement

Spanish maintains a vibrant presence in murals, churches, and local cuisine. However, a decline is observed in official signage and in certain educational spaces, a result of the pressures of gentrification.

02

Language Loss & Pride

There is strong cultural pride in family histories, but there are also documented experiences of language loss among younger generations attempting to fit in with American society.

03

Art as Resistance

Community art and murals are positioned as active tools of resistance against cultural homogenization and historical erasure.

Methodology

The study employed a qualitative and ethnographic approach, integrating Geographic Information Systems with web development to create an interactive platform that synthesizes multiple data sources.

Ethnographic Interviews

Conversations with community leaders, activists, business owners, school principals, and religious leaders.

Linguistic Landscape Analysis

Observation and analysis of the visibility of Spanish in public spaces, including signs, events, and the murals of Pilsen.

Family Histories

Collection of oral narratives to trace linguistic shifts and the preservation of traditions across generations.

ArcGIS StoryMap

Interactive mapping platform synthesizing temporal demographic data, geolocated interviews, historical points, and cultural landmarks.

Pedagogical Application

The findings were translated into a teaching model based on projects and direct experience. The courses served as living laboratories where students collected data and developed community projects alongside their coursework.

SPAN 20310

Chicago Habla Español

Approach: Transformative learning in authentic contexts where students analyze current community issues.

Content: Modules covering the history, language, food, street art, and social activism of both Pilsen (Mexican community) and Humboldt Park (Puerto Rican community).

Learning Cycle: Each module includes a pre-activity (preparation), a production activity (face-to-face), and a post-activity (field trip and analysis).

SPAN 20801

Cultura, Lengua e Identidad de Pilsen

This advanced-level course serves as an academic in-depth exploration of the research findings, focusing specifically on the Latino-Mexican community of Pilsen and its impact on Chicago's identity.

Program Structure:

  • History and Migration: Economic and political conditions that drove migration to Pilsen
  • Literary and Artistic Expressions: Local cultural production and events like the Chicago Spanish-Language Book Fair
  • Language and Integration: Multilingualism and linguistic practices between migrants and society

Final Project: Students produce a 30-minute podcast in Spanish, requiring original research, scriptwriting, and editing.
